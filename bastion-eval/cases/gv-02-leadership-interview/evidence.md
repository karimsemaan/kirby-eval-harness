# Interview Transcript: IT Leadership and Security Governance
**Interviewee:** Daniel Okafor, VP of Information Technology, Meridian Health Systems
**Interviewer:** Karim Consultant, Bastion Security
**Date:** June 2, 2026
**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Location:** Virtual (Microsoft Teams)

---

**Karim:** Daniel, thanks for making the time. Let's start with structure. Who owns cybersecurity at Meridian today?

**Daniel:** Honestly, that is the question we keep dancing around. Our Director of Information Security, Sandra Liu, left in January 2025 and **the role has been vacant since, so about seventeen months now.** Her responsibilities were split up informally. I picked up vendor and budget decisions, our GRC analyst Priya Raman picked up the risk register and audit responses, and our infrastructure manager handles firewall and patching approvals. None of that is written down anywhere. There is no RACI, and if you asked three of my managers who is accountable for security, you would get three different answers.

**Karim:** So no individual is formally accountable for cybersecurity risk?

**Daniel:** Correct. On paper it rolls up to me, but my performance goals are uptime and the EHR migration. **Security is not in my job description, and it is not in anyone else's either.**

**Karim:** Can you give me an example of how that plays out operationally?

**Daniel:** Sure. When the big ransomware advisory for healthcare came out in March, our infrastructure manager decided the patch priority on his own. There was no one to escalate to and no one whose call it formally was. It worked out, but only because he made a good judgment call.

**Karim:** Did you appoint an interim director or distribute the role formally?

**Daniel:** No. We posted the requisition in mid 2025, pulled it during the budget freeze, and only reopened it this April. In the meantime nothing was formalized. The org chart still shows the Director box with Sandra's name grayed out.

**Karim:** How is the security function resourced?

**Daniel:** Thin. We have one dedicated security engineer, plus Priya at roughly half time, supporting about 4,200 employees across six hospitals and thirty clinics. **The fiscal 2026 security operating budget was cut 30 percent from the prior year**, and the security awareness training renewal was dropped to absorb part of that cut. I have flagged the staffing shortfall to the executive team twice; both times it was deferred to the next budget cycle.

**Karim:** Let's talk about policies. What does your policy framework look like?

**Daniel:** We have fourteen information security policies. The framework document says each one is reviewed annually. In practice, **eleven of the fourteen were last reviewed in 2022 or earlier.** The remote access standard still references the Citrix gateway we decommissioned in 2024, and the acceptable use policy points employees to an intranet page that no longer exists.

**Karim:** Are the policies communicated and enforced?

**Daniel:** New hires sign the policy acknowledgment during onboarding, and that part works. But I doubt most staff could tell you where the current policies live, and we have no mechanism to check whether anyone follows them after day one.

**Karim:** How are policy exceptions handled?

**Daniel:** Informally. A department head calls me, we talk it through, and I say yes or no. **There is no exception register, nothing is documented, and nothing is time-limited.** I could not tell you today how many exceptions are in effect.

**Karim:** Who reviews whether the policies themselves are still fit for purpose?

**Daniel:** That was Sandra's job. The review calendar she maintained stopped being updated when she left, so nothing has been formally reviewed since. Priya flags the worst of it during audit prep, but she has no authority to approve policy changes.

**Karim:** Is there anything on the governance side that you feel is genuinely working?

**Daniel:** Two things. First, the Board added cybersecurity as a standing quarterly agenda item for the Audit and Compliance Committee in 2025, and this April they approved funding for the Director of Information Security search and for this external assessment. Second, our HR integration is solid. HR and IT run a joint termination checklist, badge and account access are removed the same day someone leaves, and the last internal audit verified that with zero exceptions. Security expectations are also written into onboarding for every new hire.

**Karim:** That is a useful foundation to build on. Anything else you want on the record?

**Daniel:** Just that we know the leadership vacuum is the root issue. Most of what you will find traces back to seventeen months without a security owner.

---

**Interviewer Notes:**
- Director of Information Security role vacant since January 2025. Responsibilities split informally with no documented RACI. VP confirms no individual is formally accountable for cybersecurity risk, and security appears in no one's job description.
- Resourcing is materially below need: one dedicated security engineer plus a half-time GRC analyst for 4,200 employees. FY2026 security budget cut 30 percent; awareness training renewal dropped. Shortfall raised to executives twice and deferred both times.
- Policy review cadence is broken: 11 of 14 policies last reviewed in 2022 or earlier despite a stated annual cycle. Remote access standard references a decommissioned Citrix gateway.
- Policy exceptions are granted verbally with no register, no documentation, and no expiry.
- Strengths: Board engagement (standing quarterly agenda item, funded Director search and external assessment) and HR offboarding integration (joint termination checklist, same-day revocation verified by internal audit with zero exceptions).
